Section 1
Product Overview

What is Social Stars?

Social Stars is a web-based platform providing AI-powered social-emotional learning (SEL) for autistic children aged 3–6. It operates as a three-tier ecosystem connecting children, parents, and licensed therapists on a single platform.

Children engage with six types of interactive therapeutic activities. Parents monitor progress through AI-generated insights and weekly plans. Therapists manage caseloads, assign activities, generate clinical plans, and lead live multiplayer group sessions — all from a single dashboard.

§101 Pre-Emption Strategy (All Candidates)

Under Alice Corp. v. CLS Bank (2014), software patent claims are invalid if they merely recite an abstract idea without adding an "inventive concept." To survive §101 review, all claims in this brief are drafted to:

  1. Specify execution on a non-transitory computer-readable medium or by a specifically programmed processor
  2. Recite concrete technical steps, not business outcomes
  3. Tie the claim to a specific technical problem (non-verbal input for pre-verbal children; privacy constraints on biometric data; latency requirements for therapeutic feedback loops)
  4. Avoid claiming the abstract concept broadly — claims are limited to the specific implementation described

PC-01 Body-Gesture Activity Navigation for Pre-Verbal Children HIGH STRENGTH
Plain-Language Description

The system uses the device camera to detect the child's body pose in real time using TensorFlow.js MoveNet. Recognised gestures (arms raised = "yes/confirm", wave = "next", crouch = "special action") replace touch/click navigation. This is critical for autistic children aged 3–6 who may have limited fine-motor control or verbal communication. All pose inference runs client-side; no video or skeleton data is ever sent to servers.

<80ms

Distinct gesture vocabularies mapped to therapeutic actions

Compared to touch-based navigation baselines: children requiring physical prompts to complete touch-based activities showed a ~62% reduction in required physical prompts when gesture-based navigation was available, reducing therapist/parent intervention frequency during independent activity sessions. Session completion rates for fine-motor-impaired children improved from approximately 38% to 79% in observed sessions.

Failed Obvious Approaches
❌ Approach 1: Touch-only navigation
Problem: Many autistic children ages 3–6 have fine-motor delays or sensory aversions to touch screens. Up to 80% of ASD-diagnosed children exhibit fine-motor difficulties. Touch required precise tap targeting — too demanding for this population.
❌ Approach 2: Voice command navigation
Problem: A significant subset of the target population (ages 3–6, ASD) are non-verbal or have limited expressive language. Speech recognition accuracy also degrades significantly with non-standard speech patterns common in ASD. False positive rate exceeded 40% in testing.
❌ Approach 3: Server-side pose estimation (streaming video to API)
Problem: Streaming raw video raises significant COPPA/GDPR-K compliance concerns for under-13 users. Latency added ~400ms round-trip, breaking the real-time feedback loop essential for therapeutic engagement. Client-side inference eliminates both problems.
Draft Independent Claim (§101 Hardened)
Claim 1. A non-transitory computer-readable medium storing instructions that, when executed by one or more processors of a client computing device, cause the device to perform: (a) capturing, via a camera of the client device, a continuous video stream of a user; (b) executing, entirely within the client device without transmitting any video or biometric data to a remote server, a trained skeletal pose estimation model to produce a sequence of keypoint coordinate sets, each keypoint coordinate set representing estimated positions of a plurality of body landmarks of the user in a respective video frame; (c) applying, by the processor, a rules-based gesture classifier to each keypoint coordinate set to detect one of a predefined set of gross motor gesture events comprising at least: (i) a bilateral arm-raise gesture, (ii) a unilateral wrist-sweep gesture, and (iii) a full-body crouch gesture; (d) upon detecting a gesture event, dispatching a corresponding navigation command to a state machine governing an interactive therapeutic activity without requiring touch input or voice input; and (e) transmitting only a timestamped gesture-event label, devoid of any positional coordinate data, to a remote logging server.
Best Mode & Alternative Implementations

Best mode: MoveNet Lightning model running via TensorFlow.js WebGL backend. Keypoint threshold of 0.4 confidence. 500ms debounce on gesture emission. Gesture vocabulary configurable per activity type via a JSON rules DSL stored in the activity content field.

Alternative 1: MediaPipe Pose (Google) as drop-in replacement for MoveNet — same privacy architecture, different model weights.

Alternative 2: Hand-landmark-only model (MediaPipe Hands) for fine-grained finger gestures — applicable to older children or communication board activities.

Alternative 3: Offline-first WASM deployment of pose model — allows gesture input with zero network connectivity, important for low-bandwidth clinical settings.

PC-02 Per-Child Per-Activity-Type Confusion Profile & Adaptive Difficulty Engine HIGH STRENGTH
Plain-Language Description

The system maintains a separate confusion profile for each child for each activity type (emotion, scenario, story, breathing, communication). When a child answers incorrectly, the specific wrong answer chosen is recorded alongside the correct answer — forming an error-pair. Over time, the system identifies which concepts the child systematically confuses (e.g., "always mistakes surprised for scared") and adjusts: (1) difficulty weighting to surface confused pairs more frequently, and (2) AI-generated feedback to address the specific misconception. This is fundamentally different from global difficulty adjustment — it is per-child, per-type, per-concept.

Independent confusion profiles maintained per child (one per activity type)
~54%
Reduction in repeat errors on previously-confused concept pairs after adaptive re-presentation
0.1–1.0
Continuous difficulty scale updated after every activity completion event

Failed Obvious Approaches
❌ Approach 1: Single global difficulty score per child
A child may be expert at emotion recognition but struggle with social scenarios. A global score masks these differences. Tested and observed: children with high global scores still had systematic errors in one activity type that went unaddressed.
❌ Approach 2: Therapist-manual difficulty adjustment
Requires therapist involvement for every difficulty change — not scalable beyond a handful of active clients per therapist. Also misses intra-session fluctuations.
❌ Approach 3: Simply increasing repetition of failed activities
Repeating the same activity doesn't identify the confusion pattern — a child who mistakes "surprised" for "scared" needs specifically the surprised/scared contrast surfaced, not more general emotion activities.
Draft Independent Claim (§101 Hardened)
Claim 1. A computer-implemented method for adaptive educational content delivery, executed by one or more processors, comprising: (a) maintaining, in a database, for each child user of a plurality of child users, a separate confusion profile for each of a plurality of activity types, each confusion profile comprising: (i) a set of error-pair records, each error-pair record associating a selected incorrect response with the corresponding correct response and a frequency count of how many times that specific error was made; and (ii) a difficulty score computed as a function of a sliding window of the N most-recent response correctness values for that activity type; (b) upon receiving a response submission, updating the confusion profile for the corresponding child and activity type by: incrementing the frequency count of the matching error-pair record, or creating a new error-pair record if none exists, and recomputing the difficulty score; (c) identifying, from the updated confusion profile, a highest-frequency error-pair as a priority concept pair; (d) selecting a subsequent activity item from a content pool by applying a weighted probability function that preferentially surfaces activity items addressing the identified priority concept pair; and (e) transmitting the selected activity item to the child user's client device.
Best Mode & Alternative Implementations

Best mode: Confusion matrix stored as JSON in a PostgreSQL JSONB column on ChildProfile. Sliding window of 10 attempts. Priority threshold of 3 repeated errors. 70/30 split between prioritised and random selection.

Alternative 1: Machine-learning-based selection (e.g., multi-armed bandit or knowledge tracing model) instead of rule-based priority. Preserves claim scope while increasing sophistication.

Alternative 2: Export confusion matrix to therapist dashboard as a visual "heat map" — distinct patentable UI embodiment.

PC-03 Privacy-Preserving Group Emotion Detection for Live Therapeutic Sessions HIGH STRENGTH
Plain-Language Description

During Live Circle Time group sessions, the system detects each participant's facial emotion in real time using face-api.js running entirely in the browser. PC-04 Mood-State-Gated Adaptive Activity Routing Algorithm MEDIUM STRENGTH
Plain-Language Description

When a child logs in, they complete a mood check-in by selecting from 8 illustrated emotion icons. The system's routing algorithm uses the detected mood state to algorithmically modify the composition and ordering of the child's activity queue for that session. Specifically, the algorithm applies mood-state-specific weight multipliers to activity-type affinity scores, then executes a weighted sort to produce a personalised session plan. Dysregulating moods (anxious, angry) cause the algorithm to elevate breathing activity priority score to the front of the queue and temporarily suppress cognitively-demanding activity types.

Technical Framing (§101 Compliance)

This is a technical algorithmic method, not a business rule. The invention lies in: (1) the specific data structure representing mood-to-activity-type affinity weights; (2) the weighted sorting algorithm applied to the activity pool; and (3) the real-time modification of the content delivery queue without human intervention. The technical problem solved is automatically adapting a digital therapeutic content delivery sequence based on a computationally-detected emotional state, which requires specific algorithmic steps implemented by a processor.

~67%

Failed Obvious Approaches
❌ Approach 1: Fixed daily activity schedule (same order every session)
Children with ASD have highly variable emotional regulation from day to day. A fixed schedule ignores emotional state, leading to attempted high-demand activities during dysregulated states — a known trigger for meltdowns in clinical literature.
❌ Approach 2: Therapist manually prescribes activity for each session
Requires synchronous therapist availability before each child session. Not scalable and impractical for daily home use where therapist is unavailable.
Draft Independent Claim (§101 Hardened)
Claim 1. A computer-implemented method for dynamically ordering a therapeutic activity queue, executed by one or more processors, comprising: (a) receiving, from a user interface executing on a client device, a mood state input selecting one mood label from a predefined discrete mood vocabulary; (b) retrieving, from a database, a mood-weight table associating each mood label in the vocabulary with a vector of numerical priority weights, each weight corresponding to one of a plurality of predefined therapeutic activity types; (c) retrieving, from the database, a set of available activity records, each record comprising an activity type identifier and content data; (d) computing, by the processor, a composite priority score for each activity record by multiplying the activity-type weight from the mood-weight vector by one or more additional personalisation weight factors derived from a per-user confusion profile; and (e) generating an ordered activity delivery queue by sorting the activity records in descending order of their computed composite priority scores, and transmitting the ordered queue to the client device for sequential presentation to the user.

Alternative 1: Continuous mood re-assessment mid-session using facial emotion detection (PC-03) to dynamically reorder the queue without requiring explicit check-in.

Alternative 2: Parent or therapist overrides mood state from their respective dashboards.

PC-05 Cross-Role Real-Time Therapeutic Session Notification & Schedule Synchronisation MEDIUM STRENGTH
Plain-Language Description

When a therapist initiates a live group therapy session, the system automatically identifies all parent accounts whose children are linked to that therapist via a relational database query, then fans out a real-time join-code notification to each parent's active browser session simultaneously via individualised WebSocket channels. The parent receives a persistent session banner with a one-click join action without needing to poll or refresh. A separate weekly schedule synchronisation system allows the therapist to publish a recurring timetable that is automatically mirrored to all linked parent dashboards.

Technical Framing (§101 Compliance)

The technical problem solved is the automated resolution of a many-to-many therapist-to-family relationship graph at session-start time and the fan-out delivery of session state to individualised persistent push channels. The invention combines a specific database traversal algorithm (therapist → family → parent), a channel-naming scheme derived from parent user IDs, and a real-time push architecture — all implemented by server-side processors executing defined algorithmic steps.

PC-06 Non-Blocking Streamed AI Coaching with Therapeutic Persona Layer HIGH STRENGTH
Plain-Language Description

After each activity completion, the child receives immediate encouraging feedback from an AI-powered "Social Coach" character. The system queries an LLM (Claude Haiku) via a streaming API, so the first words of the response appear within ~300ms rather than waiting for the full response to generate (~2–3 seconds). A persona layer maps raw LLM output through age-appropriate vocabulary filters and character-consistent speech patterns before display.

Failed Obvious Approaches
❌ Approach 1: Blocking AI call (await full response before advancing)
LLM generation takes 1.5–3 seconds. Children aged 3–6 have attention spans of 3–5 minutes total for digital tasks. A 2-second wait after every activity is a 40%+ overhead per session — directly reducing therapeutic dose achieved per session.
❌ Approach 2: Pre-generated canned feedback messages
Cannot address specific confusion (e.g., child confused "surprised" with "scared"). Generic positive feedback ("Great job!") has lower therapeutic value than targeted feedback addressing the specific error made. Requires manual authoring of thousands of activity-outcome combinations.
❌ Approach 3: AI response without persona filtering

Petition Narrative — Original Contribution of Major Significance

The petitioner has developed Social Stars, a novel platform representing an original and significant contribution to the fields of therapeutic artificial intelligence and accessible human-computer interaction. The platform introduces multiple patentable inventions that address longstanding unsolved technical challenges in delivering evidence-based autism interventions at scale.

Prior to this work, digital therapeutic platforms for autistic children relied on static, pre-authored content sequences with no adaptive personalisation, required synchronous in-person or video clinician involvement for progress adjustment, and were unable to leverage biometric signals (such as facial emotion and body gesture) without significant privacy compliance barriers. The petitioner's inventions directly address each of these barriers through novel technical contributions.

The petitioner's Privacy-Preserving Group Emotion Detection system (PC-03) is an original contribution resolving a fundamental conflict in the field: the clinical value of real-time emotional state monitoring versus the legal impossibility of transmitting biometric data for children under COPPA. No prior art system achieves both simultaneously through client-side neural inference with server-side label aggregation. This architecture has implications beyond autism therapy — it establishes a replicable pattern for privacy-compliant biometric monitoring applicable across all paediatric digital health applications.

The petitioner's Per-Child Per-Type Confusion Mapping engine (PC-02) introduces a granular error-pair data model not found in any known prior art in adaptive educational technology. Existing systems adapt difficulty globally or per-subject; this invention maintains independent confusion profiles per activity type, enabling identification of specific conceptual confusions (e.g., consistent misidentification of "surprised" as "scared") that would be invisible to global difficulty scoring. This enables a qualitatively different form of personalisation with documented therapeutic impact.

The petitioner's Body-Gesture Navigation system (PC-01) applies skeletal pose estimation — a technology previously limited to gaming and sports analytics — to the specific accessibility problem of non-verbal pre-verbal children interacting with therapeutic software. This represents an original cross-domain transfer of a technology to a population for which no prior digital therapeutic platform has provided gesture-based interaction.
